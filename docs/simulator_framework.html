01

What it produces

The output defines the whole design — everything upstream exists to make these numbers honest.

Given two rosters, a date, and a venue, one run of the simulator returns a full distribution of outcomes, not a single prediction:

Game level

Win probability & score

Home/away win probability, the full margin distribution (not just a point spread), and the total-points distribution — so we read out spread-cover and over/under probabilities directly, with calibrated uncertainty.

Player level

Every stat line, with intervals

Each player's points, rebounds, assists, minutes, shooting, etc. as a distribution — median plus percentiles — reflecting matchup, role, and rotation, not a season average.

Because it simulates the actual game, the same engine answers questions a regression can't: what if this player is out, what if the pace is faster, how does the fourth quarter swing. That flexibility is the reason for a simulator over a direct stat model.

The design principle: keep facts, ratings, and simulation strictly separated. The engines never touch raw data — only the calibrated ratings — so we can swap a better rating in (e.g. RAPM-WAR for box-WAR) without rewriting the simulator, and re-run history leakage-free.

03

The eight engines

Each is an independent, testable module with a defined input (ratings) and output (game state changes).

A

Player representation

Turns each player into an ability vector: usage rate, shot-location distribution, finishing skill (points over expected), passing/assist propensity, turnover rate, offensive/defensive rebound rates, foul rates (drawn & committed), free-throw%, and a defensive profile (defender quality, rim protection). Every value carries an uncertainty band and is aged & projected to the game date.

projectionsxPTSdefender-quality
B

Rotation engine — who's on the floor

Predicts the on-court five for each team through the game: minutes projection, realistic stint structure (starter runs, bench units, staggering of stars), foul-trouble response, blowout / garbage-time logic, and rest on back-to-backs. Parameters are learned from historical rotations reconstructed by the stint solver. This is the behavioral heart — and the hardest module.

rotation-modelminutes-projectionstints
C

Assignment engine — who guards whom

Given the two on-court fives, predicts the defensive matchup matrix (primary assignments, cross-matches, switch tendencies) from size/position logic plus historical matchup tendencies. Feeds shot difficulty and the defensive-pressure term on each offensive player.

matchup-datadefender-quality
D

Possession engine — the core

The per-possession decision process: pace/clock → who initiates (usage-weighted, matchup-adjusted) → action selection (shot / drive / pass / turnover / drawn foul) → if a shot, sample location then resolve make/miss from xPTS × shooter-skill × defender-contest → rebound contest → assist, and-1, free throws. Emits box-score events and updates the score.

xPTSRAPMdefender-qualitytendencies
E

Game-state manager

Tracks period, game clock, shot clock, score, team fouls / bonus, timeouts, and possession arrow — and handles special situations: end-of-quarter 2-for-1s and heaves, late-game intentional fouling and 3-for-2 math, and overtime. These situations disproportionately drive close-game outcomes.

clock-modelfoul-model
F

Correlation & realism layer

Real games have game-script and hot/cold streaks; independent possessions under-disperse the outcome distribution. This layer injects controlled correlation — a per-game team-form random effect and mild possession autocorrelation — so the simulated spread of results matches reality, not just the mean.

variance-calibration
G

Monte Carlo orchestrator

Runs the game N times (10k+), collecting outcomes into distributions. Uses variance-reduction (common random numbers, antithetic variates) so probabilities stabilize with fewer sims — important for running a full slate nightly for the website.

simulation-core
H

Calibration & constraints

Enforces the accounting that keeps a simulation honest: team minutes sum to 240, usage sums to 100%, pace and team totals land in real ranges. Then post-calibrates outputs (isotonic / Platt) so the win probabilities are actually calibrated, and shrinks every noisy input toward its prior.

shrinkagecalibration
04

The possession loop

One trip down the floor — the atom the whole simulator is built from. Repeated ~200× per game, ~10k× per matchup.

1

Possession start & pace

Set the clock burn for this possession by sampling from the two teams' pace tendencies and the game state.

state in: score game clock shot clock on-court 10
2

Initiator

Choose who uses the possession — usage-weighted among the five on offense, adjusted for the defensive assignment (a star smothered by an elite stopper cedes some usage).

3

Action selection

Sample the action from the initiator's tendencies, conditioned on defender quality, shot clock, and score/time: shoot · drive · pass-out · turnover · draw foul.

branch → turnover (forced vs unforced) ends possession; foul → free throws
4

Shot resolution

If a shot: sample the location from the shooter's shot chart, then resolve make/miss.

P(make) = xPTS(location) × shooter skill (POE) × defender contest (DefQ + pressure)
5

Rebound contest

On a miss, contest the board — probability split by the on-court players' offensive/defensive rebound rates. Offensive board → new possession, same team, reset shot clock.

6

Bookkeeping & state update

Credit the box-score events (points, assist, rebound, block, steal, foul, FTs), advance the clock and score, update team fouls / bonus, and hand off possession. Loop back to step 1.

Why this granularity
Modeling the possession — rather than projecting box lines directly — is what makes the interactions real: a good defender lowers make probability at the moment of the shot, foul trouble changes who is even on the floor, and pace emerges from the two teams rather than being assumed.
05

Two simulation modes

We build both and let the backtest decide — the same bake-off discipline we use for WAR.

Mode 1 · Milestone

Distributional simulation

Project each player's stat-line distribution for the matchup (mean + variance), impose a realistic covariance structure (teammates' usage trades off, team scoring correlates), and sample jointly. Reuses the projection + matchup work directly.

  • Pros: robust, fast, no compounding error, easy to calibrate.
  • Cons: no in-game dynamics (foul trouble, game script, clutch).
Mode 2 · Full

Possession simulation

The full engine in §3–4: play every possession. Richest possible output — any lineup, any what-if, real in-game dynamics.

  • Pros: maximum realism & flexibility, true stat-line detail.
  • Cons: compounding error, hard to calibrate, heavier compute.

Strategy: ship the distributional mode first as a robust, testable baseline, then build the possession engine, and bake them off on out-of-sample predictive accuracy. We never bet the whole project on the hardest version working perfectly — the simpler model is both a milestone and a floor.

07

Build sequence

Ingredients first, then the two modes, then calibration. Each phase is independently useful.

0FOUND.

Foundations

Stint reconstruction (solver A) → RAPM → defender quality → finish the projection system (aging, rosters, rookies). The ratings the engines consume.

1MODE 1

Distributional simulator

Project player stat-line distributions + covariance; sample jointly to get win prob + stat lines. First end-to-end predictions. Validate & calibrate.

2CORE

Possession engine (fixed lineups)

Build the possession loop (§4) with lineups held fixed. Validate that simulated aggregate stats — pace, team points, usage splits — match reality.

3DYNAMIC

Rotation + assignment engines

Add the behavioral layers: dynamic lineups over the game and defensive matchups. Now the sim reacts to foul trouble and matchups.

4REALISM

Game-state + correlation

Clutch situations, intentional fouling, OT, and the correlation layer so the outcome distribution has the right spread.

5SHIP

Monte Carlo, calibration, bake-off

Full orchestration, probability calibration, leakage-free backtest, and the mode-1-vs-mode-2 bake-off. Then productionize for the website (precompute the nightly slate).

08

Validation & metrics

A simulator is only as good as its calibration. We validate it three ways, all leakage-free (walk-forward, simulate each game using only prior data).

Outcome accuracy

  • Win prob: Brier, log-loss, reliability curve, ECE — and vs. the market baseline / closing line (CLV).
  • Score: MAE on final margin & total.

Distributional honesty

  • Interval coverage: do actual player stat lines land inside the predicted 50/80/90% intervals at the right rate?
  • Spread: is the simulated score variance realistic (not under-dispersed)?

Aggregate realism (the sanity gate)

Before trusting any prediction, the sim must reproduce the league it was built from: pace distribution, points-per-100, usage curves, shot-location mix, rebound rates, and the shape of player stat distributions. If simulated seasons don't look like real ones, nothing downstream is trustworthy.

09

Honest challenges

Where this is genuinely hard — named up front, because pretending otherwise is how simulators quietly fail.

Compounding error
Small per-possession biases stack over ~200 possessions. The outcome model has to be well-calibrated at the possession level, or the game drifts. This is the single biggest risk, and the reason we validate aggregate realism before trusting outcomes.
Rotation realism
Predicting coach substitution behavior is the messiest, most human part of the model. It won't be perfect; we bound the damage by validating minutes distributions and letting the distributional mode (which needs only minutes totals, not patterns) serve as a floor.
Under-dispersion
Independent possessions produce outcome distributions that are too narrow — the model looks overconfident. The correlation layer (§3F) exists specifically to fix this, and interval-coverage checks catch it.
Defensive attribution & data limits
Individual defense carries irreducible uncertainty, matchup data only reaches 2017-18, and per-shot defender identity doesn't exist publicly. The engine is designed to degrade gracefully — it uses aggregate defender quality where per-event data is missing.
10

State-of-the-art edges

Where we push past a standard simulator, once the foundations are in.

Hierarchical Bayesian shrinkage

Every rating is a posterior, not a point estimate — small samples pull toward position/role priors. The simulator samples from these posteriors, so uncertainty propagates all the way to the final intervals.

Player-style embeddings

Learn low-dimensional style vectors so matchup interactions generalize — how a shooter fares vs. a rim-protector — beyond the pairs actually observed in the matchup data.

Fatigue from tracking

Use speed/distance tracking to model within-game and schedule fatigue, feeding both the rotation engine and late-game shot efficiency.

Scenario toggles

Because it's a real simulation, we can flip a player out (injury), cap minutes (load management), or force a pace — and get a full re-simulated distribution. This is the website's killer feature.

The through-line
Every earlier piece — projections, xPTS, WAR, RAPM, defender quality, defensive pressure, stints, matchup data — is not a side project. Each one is an input to this engine. The simulator is where the whole pipeline finally pays off as a single, coherent product.
